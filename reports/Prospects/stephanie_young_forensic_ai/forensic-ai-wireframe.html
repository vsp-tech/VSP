📖 Dusky Standard — Full Text▼
Dusky v. United States, 362 U.S. 402 (1960):

"It is not enough for the district judge to find that 'the defendant [is] oriented to time and place and [has] some recollection of events,' but that the test must be whether he has sufficient present ability to consult with his lawyer with a reasonable degree of rational understanding — and whether he has a rational as well as factual understanding of the proceedings against him."

California codification: PC §1367 — a defendant is mentally incompetent if, as a result of mental disorder or developmental disability, the defendant is unable to understand the nature of the criminal proceedings or to assist counsel in the conduct of a defense in a rational manner.

MacCAT-CA
22 items
MacArthur Competence Assessment Tool — Criminal Adjudication · Poythress, Nicholson, Otto, et al. (1999)
Primary structured interview for competency to stand trial. Three subscales: Understanding (knowledge of legal proceedings), Reasoning (ability to process case-relevant information), Appreciation (application of understanding to own case). Validated for use across diagnoses including psychosis and intellectual disability.
When to use: Primary instrument for all PC §1368 CST evaluations.
ECST-R
28 items
Evaluation of Competency to Stand Trial — Revised · Rogers, Tillbrook & Sewell (2004)
Structured clinical interview with 4 scales: Consult with Counsel, Factual Understanding of Courtroom Proceedings, Rational Understanding of Courtroom Proceedings, and Atypical Presentation scale (feigning screen). Provides T-scores by impairment level.
When to use: Supplemental to MacCAT-CA; especially useful when atypical presentation (feigning) is a concern.
Competency Screening Test (CST)
22 items
Lipsitt, Lelos & McGarry (1971)
Sentence-completion format screening instrument for competency. Historically important but largely superseded by MacCAT-CA. Less psychometrically robust; not recommended as primary instrument in current practice. May be used in initial screening contexts.
When to use: Historical reference; some jurisdictions use for initial screen only.
CAST-MR
50 items
Competence Assessment for Standing Trial for Defendants with Mental Retardation · Everington & Luckasson (1992)
Specifically designed for defendants with intellectual disability. Three sections: Basic Legal Concepts (T/F), Skills to Assist Defense (open-ended), Understanding Case Events (defendant-specific). Language adapted for concrete cognitive functioning.
When to use: When defendant has IQ <70 or intellectual disability documented — use CAST-MR instead of or alongside MacCAT-CA.
R-CRAS
30 items
Rogers Criminal Responsibility Assessment Scales · Rogers (1984)
Structured rating instrument for criminal responsibility evaluations. Five scales: Patient Reliability, Organicity, Psychopathology, Cognitive Control, Behavioral Control. Maps to M'Naghten, ALI, and Guilty But Mentally Ill (GBMI) standards. Clinician-rated from records and interview data.
When to use: MSO evaluations (PC §1026); provides systematic quantification of psychopathology at time of offense.
MMPI-2-RF
338 items
Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory — 2 Restructured Form · Ben-Porath & Tellegen (2008)
Comprehensive self-report psychopathology measure. 51 scales including 9 validity indicators, 9 restructured clinical scales (RC Scales), Higher-Order Scales, and Specific Problems scales. Critical for forensic use: F-r (infrequent responses), Fp-r (infrequent psychopathology), RBS (response bias). RC6 (paranoia), RC8 (aberrant experiences) relevant for psychosis presentations.
When to use: Both CST and MSO when psychopathology profile and response validity documentation needed.
PAI
344 items
Personality Assessment Inventory · Morey (1991)
Self-report psychopathology and personality measure. 22 full scales, 31 subscales. Key forensic scales: PAR (paranoia), SCZ (schizophrenia), ALC/DRG (substance use), AGG (aggression), SUI (suicidal ideation). Validity indicators: NIM (negative impression), PIM (positive impression), MAL (malingering index), RDF (Rogers Discriminant Function).
When to use: Alternative or supplement to MMPI-2-RF; lower reading level required (~4th grade). Preferred when defendant has limited literacy.
TOMM
50 items/trial
Test of Memory Malingering · Tombaugh (1996)
Performance validity test for cognitive feigning. Three trials: Trial 1, Trial 2, Retention Trial (30-min delay). Cutoff: ≤45 on any trial suggests feigning. High sensitivity and specificity. Requires no prior cognitive assessment; can be administered to low-functioning individuals.
When to use: First instrument administered in any forensic evaluation to establish response validity before competency or cognitive measures.
SIMS
75 items
Structured Inventory of Malingered Symptomatology · Smith & Burger (1997)
Self-report screen for feigned psychiatric and cognitive symptoms. Five subscales: Low Intelligence (LI), Affective Disorders (AF), Neurological Impairment (NI), Psychosis (P), Amnestic Disorders (AM). Cutoff score of 14 or above suggests feigning. Less robust psychometrically than TOMM; use as part of multi-method validity battery.
When to use: When symptom validity (not memory validity) is the primary concern; complement TOMM.
HCR-20 V3
20 items
Historical Clinical Risk Management — 20, Version 3 · Douglas, Hart, Webster & Belfrage (2013)
Structured Professional Judgment (SPJ) guide for violence risk assessment. Three domains: Historical (10 items, static), Clinical (5 items, dynamic current), Risk Management (5 items, contextual). Does not produce a score; produces a structured professional judgment of low/moderate/high risk with case formulation. Widely used internationally and in California courts.
When to use: Violence risk assessments for criminal cases, SVP (violence history), conditional release evaluations.
PCL-R
20 items
Psychopathy Checklist — Revised · Hare (2003)
Gold standard measure of psychopathy. Clinician-rated from interview and records. Two factors: Factor 1 (Interpersonal-Affective: grandiosity, callousness, manipulation) and Factor 2 (Antisocial Lifestyle: impulsivity, criminal versatility). Scores: 0–40; ≥30 = Psychopathy. Highest scores predict violent and sexual recidivism. Requires extensive training to administer reliably.
When to use: Violence risk, SVP evaluations, capital sentencing mitigation (opposing counsel), conditional release hearings.
VRAG-R
12 items
Violence Risk Appraisal Guide — Revised · Rice, Harris & Lang (2013)
Actuarial violence risk instrument. 12 items including PCL-R score, age at index offense, elementary school maladjustment, DSM diagnosis, and victim characteristics. Provides a score associated with violent recidivism base rates over 5 and 12 year follow-up periods. Strongest evidence base for general violent recidivism.
When to use: Violence risk in SVP and conditional release contexts; population of NGRI acquittees.
LSI-R
54 items
Level of Service Inventory — Revised · Andrews & Bonta (1995)
General recidivism risk/need instrument. 10 subscales: Criminal History, Education/Employment, Financial, Family/Marital, Accommodation, Leisure/Recreation, Companions, Alcohol/Drug Problem, Emotional/Personal, Attitudes/Orientation. Widely used in probation/parole decisions. Less specific to violent or sexual offending; identifies criminogenic needs.
When to use: General recidivism, sentencing evaluation, treatment planning, parole risk assessment.
Static-99R
10 items
Static-99 Revised · Helmus, Thornton, Hanson & Babchishin (2012)
Most widely used actuarial instrument for sexual recidivism risk. 10 static (historical) items: age at release, relationship history, prior sentencing, victim characteristics, offense type. Produces a total score corresponding to normative risk categories: Low (≤1), Low-Moderate (2–3), Moderate (4–5), High (6+). Age correction reduces scores for older offenders. Uses routine correction norms for most SVP cases.
When to use: All SVP evaluations (WI §6600); required in California SVP proceedings alongside dynamic risk measure.
STABLE-2007
13 items
Stable Dynamic Risk Factors for Sexual Offenders · Hanson, Harris, Scott & Helmus (2007)
Dynamic (changeable) risk factor measure for sexual recidivism. 13 items across 5 domains: Significant Social Influences, Intimacy Deficits, Sexual Self-Regulation, General Self-Regulation, Cooperation with Supervision. Clinician-rated from interview and collateral records. Combined with Static-99R to produce overall risk classification. Must be administered within 6 months of conditional release.
When to use: SVP conditional release evaluations; combined scoring with Static-99R mandated in California DSH practice.
Acute-2007
7 items
Acute Dynamic Risk Factors for Sexual Offenders · Hanson, Harris, Scott & Helmus (2007)
Acute (rapidly changing) dynamic risk measure. 7 items: Victim Access, Hostility, Sexual Preoccupation, Rejection of Supervision, Emotional Collapse, Collapse of Social Supports, Substance Abuse. Scores change rapidly (days to weeks) and signal immediate recidivism risk. Used alongside STABLE-2007 for comprehensive dynamic risk assessment.
When to use: Acute risk monitoring during conditional release; community supervision decisions for SVP conditionally released individuals.
JSORRAT-II
12 items
Juvenile Sex Offender Recidivism Risk Assessment Tool — II · Epperson et al. (2006)
Actuarial risk instrument for juvenile sexual offenders. 12 items derived from a Utah juvenile sex offender sample. Provides a raw score converted to risk categories. Less normative data than Static-99R; primarily validated with U.S. juvenile adjudicated sex offense populations. Used when SVP petition involves offending that began in adolescence.
When to use: SVP evaluations where qualifying offense was committed as a juvenile; supplement to adult actuarial instruments.
