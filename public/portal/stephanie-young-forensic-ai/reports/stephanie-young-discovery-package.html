Forensic Evaluation Workflow — As Stephanie Described It

This is the workflow the product must support. Each phase has distinct pain points and AI opportunities.

Phase 1: Records Review

  • Receives case documents from referring party (court, attorney, CPS)
  • Documents: medical records, court updates, prior psych evals, rap sheets, police reports
  • Some scanned from the 1980s — handwritten police reports, degraded quality
  • Combs through all data for holes, inconsistencies, missing sources
  • Must track down missing originals when cited data has no source
Pain: "These are documents that come to you scanned sometimes from like the 80s. Very difficult to read. Sometimes police reports were handwritten."
— Stephanie

Phase 2: Clinical Interview (1–4 hours)

  • Face-to-face with defendant/patient/subject
  • Simultaneously: building rapport, asking questions, thinking of follow-ups, AND typing verbatim quotes
  • Must capture exact wording — cited in reports, challenged in court
  • After questions: administer standardized tests (neuropsych, personality, forensic assessment instruments)
  • Recording permitted with consent
Pain: "I'm sitting there typing away furiously, trying to get exact verbatim quotes while listening and building a rapport and also thinking of what question should I ask next. It's a lot to juggle."
— Stephanie

Phase 3: Report Writing (15–22+ pages)

  • Structure: referring question → sources → methodology → history → interview → tests → analysis → conclusions
  • History section is "a bear" — synthesizing records into coherent narrative with citations
  • Every paragraph cited. Verbatim quotes placed throughout.
  • Must be "beyond reproach" for court submission
Pain: "Based on the legal and ethical standards of psychologists, it has to be beyond reproach, a very well written, accurate report. And that can be incredibly time consuming. I just finished one yesterday that was 22 pages long."
— Stephanie

Phase 4: Testimony

  • Subpoenaed to defend every statement on the stand
  • Opposing counsel challenges specific citations and conclusions
  • If AI hallucinated a citation, she gets destroyed under cross-examination

The Hallucination Incident

Bastion GPT generated Spanish norms for a standardized test with multiple fabricated journal article citations. Stephanie knew the literature and had never seen these sources. She spent hours tracking down every paper by the supposed author — the articles didn't exist.

This was for a case where she expected to testify. She stopped using Bastion entirely for a period. This incident makes citation traceability the #1 non-negotiable requirement for any tool she uses.

"Bastion came up with multiple sources and gave me output scores for these made up data sources. I spent a very long time literally trying to track down the author, every single journal article he'd ever written — and he didn't. I was just freaked out."
— Stephanie
